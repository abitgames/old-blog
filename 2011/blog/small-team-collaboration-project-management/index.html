Small Team Collaboration: How To Choose Project Management Web App

Written on May 2, 2011 by nantas in Blog

When we decided to form a remote dev team, the first thing came to mind is how to set up a workflow that makes remote collaboration possible and efficient. We did a lot of research and field test to find a perfect workflow for a small remote team, and we never stop looking for better options if there’re flaws in the old one. In this series of article I will share the experience and information we got on how to set up a good workflow for a small dev team. Some of the information are more helpful to remote teams.

This is the first one of the series, which focuses on how to choose a Project Management (PM) web app for a small team (that develops indie game). By small team I mean team consist of 2-5 developers. So here we go.

What Features Are We Looking For?

If my team would share the same office at work, then essentially a big white board and some ways to store and accessing documents should be good enough for a team of 3 members. Unfortunately that’s not possible for us. If your team is collaborating remotely, you need a way to keep everyone on the same page, including features like making plans, checking schedule, discuss dev topics and tasks tracking.

Most importantly, it should be a web app or service that can be accessed from any computer. A web app can help you skip the trouble of installing and updating PM softwares on all computers your team using. Also I don’t think anybody works without a web browser today, it’s just much easier and faster to use web apps when your browser is already open.

Secondly watch out for the cost. With hundreds of free web apps or free plans out there on the internet, you should be able to find something you need without paying a dime. Most of the charged service are designed for medium to large teams (10+ devs) and really rich(also heavy) in features. This leads me to the next point:

Always choose solutions Small teams need light weight solution. Simply because light weight PM web-app are easier to adapt, and faster to load and update. The essential features small dev teams should look for are: dashboard, milestone, calendar, task assignment, email notification and a way to keep online documents. I will go into details in the next part.

Web Apps We Have Used

Our first PM web-app is Trac. It’s a powerful open-sourced PM web-app that needs to be installed and run on your own host. Once it’s set up and running, it’s mainly a wiki with issue tracking system. The Trac wiki has very good markups makes your document writing and indexing very easy. The issue tracking system works fine, you can categorize and prioritize tickets as you want. It also supports a customizable full ticket state workflow such as new->assigned->resolved->closed(->reopened). But Trac has problem handling too many tickets, you cannot modify multiple tickets at the same time – at least when we were still using it.

Another big issue with Trac is that there’s no dashboard, you have to update your wiki front page manually  to simulate one. Also we have to use Zoho Discuss to post discussion and media files.

One day our programmer Karas decide to upgrade our PM system from Trac to Redmine. They are pretty similar. But Redmine has more features such as dashboard, forum, nice integration with Git(the version control system we use for our projects).

I think Redmine is better than Trac just because the build-in dashboard. A dashboard let you keep your teammates noticed of what’s your latest change to the build, show your tasks and bugs at the first place after you logged in. And you can customize the dashboard to include any handy links or information you need. It’s very helpful to keep the team on the same page and get around your project information quickly.

But soon it turns out that the all-in-one package comes with a cost, it takes longer time to load a certain page in your browser. The idea about having a single system that handles all features looks charming at first. But if the one mega system is slow on switching pages, opening posts and etc, a combination of some lightweight, fast tool may serve you better. I’m not calling Redmine out because it’s slower than other feature rich system. Actually from our experience, the more features a PM system has, the slower it can be.

Why Speed Matters?

And why the page loading speed is a big deal?You just need to wait a bit longer for each request, get used to it, wouldn’t you?

The problem leans more towards sub-conscious level. If you’re using game engine which runs slow, you will get used to it because that’s the only way to do your work(assume your team don’t want to change engine). But you can totally do your work in a small dev team without the help of a project management system. The PM system is there for better planning, task tracking and keeping reference. If a system is slow and you hate to wait, you may subconsciously choose not to use it in some circumstances.

For example you found an issue when playtest your game, it needs to be fixed by another team member. You are supposed to enter a ticket in the PM system so everything can be noticed, prioritized and tracked. But since it takes too long to navigate to and load the create ticket page, you decide to drop your team mate a message on IM (in our case, Gtalk) instead. Unfortunately your team mate is too busy to read the message carefully or he read but then forget about it.

That’s not the worst case, the worse case is that your team mate drops what he’s doing and jump to the issue you told him immediately. He fixes it as soon as possible because he’s afraid that he may forget it. You not only interrupted your team mates work, causing him to lose efficiency, but more importantly from there on, you will more likely use direct message than enter a ticket to keep everything on record and manageable, which misses the whole point of a PM system.

Zoho Collaboration Family

So after Redmine turns out to be slowing down the process, we start to try other light web-app. Zoho has a really nice products line including Projects, Docs, Discussions, Wiki and Planner. You can choose the product you need and start using them with no real effort. Once you find a Zoho product less useful, you can just drop it while keep using other web-apps. Besides you can register and sign in with your google, facebook or yahoo account, which makes start working everyday a bit easier.
We have tried Zoho Discussions, Planner and Docs(as an alternative when google doc can’t be accessed from China). Zoho Discussions is the best discussion web-app for small teams I’ve ever tried. Never bother get yourself a vBulletin or phpBB if you only got 3-5 users. It’s just not worth the effort, besides a full sized forum software is much slower than a simple discussion web-app. All the feature you need is posting and replying topics, insert image from your desktop or from URL. That’s all. And you only need one forum, sub-forum doesn’t make sense if you only have 1-2 posts total per day.

Zoho Planner is about the perfect size for prototyping a new concept. You can put all the information you need into one page, no need to browse around or wait for page loading. But after full production started, you will need better way to arrange lots of tasks or issues. I have to remind you, Zoho Planner hasn’t been updated for quite a while. Maybe Zoho dev team already forget about it.

The only product from Zoho that provides task tracking is Zoho Projects. It’s a very solid PM web-app with essential features as Redmine, such as dashboard, tasks, milestones, calendar, forum and wiki. We didn’t choose Zoho Projects at the first place is because back then there’s no wiki integration for Zoho Projects, and we really want to use wiki to arrange our documents and information. And now it makes a lot of sense to choose Zoho Projects as an all-in-one package for your PM purposes. Also it’s relatively faster than Redmine.

From All-in-one To Combos

When we were still trying to find a perfect PM solution. Github released a big update which contains a mini task and milestone tracking system. It’s very light weight and fast. Since we need to keep the Github repository page open most of the time, it makes sense to transfer our task tracking needs to Github as well. Then we need a place to arrange document and store other information. We are still big fans of wiki although I believe wiki works best for team with 5+ people.

Anyway this time we chose MediaWiki for the purpose. It support rich media, has very powerful wiki markups that can help you create beautiful documents with ease. And more importantly, it’s much faster than other wikis with task tracking function (such as Redmine and Trac).

For discussion purposes, we actually use Flickr since we found most of the time a discussion with only text is not enough. We actually upgraded our workflow to communicate mainly with pictures, and word comments just for the assist. I will go into more interesting details about the topic in the future posts.

The above three (Github, MediaWiki, Flickr) is our current project management workflow. We are feeling comfortable with this set. And since it’s a combination of tools, we can easily switch out parts that we don’t need anymore or add new web-app into the workflow anytime. I think a combination of tools and all-in-one package both have its own strength. It’s really important that you try out the workflow and see if you’re comfortable with.

A List Of Recommended Web Apps

Here I will provide you a list of recommendation for PM web apps, each with a short comment. I didn’t use every one of them for real project, but have done a little research for each. In this list, anything with 10$+ monthly fee is excluded, or at least the listed ones have a decent free plan for small team.

All-in-one Packages

Redmine: One of the most powerful PM web-app. It has a lot of features and tons of plugin for extension. If you need some advanced features such as repository integration, Redmine is going to be a good solution. Beware that you have to use your own host to run the application. (Geek power needed!)

Zoho Projects: A light weight all-in-one PM web-app. It’s more friendly to non-programmers because everything is dead simple. It also includes a wiki if you prefer that over docs. The only drawback is that free users are only allowed to create one project. You may need to separate different projects by milestones instead.

TeamLab: Another feature rich PM web app. It has a community portal which features blog, bookmark and wiki. The projects portal acts just like above two with different styles. It has a excellent dashboard. But I don’t like it when logged in, it always put you in community portal, which is still far away from tasks and milestones.

TeamWorkPM: Similar stuff, but with a very clean and elegant UI. I think this web app would work great for small team because how simple it is. Also worth mentioning it has a billing tab that keeps track of expenses and income that may fit some teams’ need.

All the above web-apps have work time tracking feature that allows you to keep note of how many hours each team member put into certain issues. This is pretty handy if your team use working hours to measure payments.

Individual Tools That Can Be Combined Freely

Then here’re web apps that focus on a certain feature for you to combine:

MediaWiki: Our programmer just love it. If you ever need a standalone wiki system for whatever reason, MediaWiki won’t let you down. The edit page has some quick buttons that makes page editing possible for people who don’t want to learn wiki markups. It also requires installation on your own host so be prepared. (Geek power needed!)

Github(issue tracking): Actually it’s a 2-for-1 situation. If your team happens to use Git as your version control system, there’s no reason not to use Github’s issue tracking feature especially when you are looking for light weight solution. It also automatically create link to a certain commit, makes feature implementing and bug fixing easy to verify.

Zoho Discussions: Best discussion web-app for small team. No need for installation, fast access, able to post pictures from your desktop or URL. Also it can give you email notification so you won’t miss any point. Simple is beautiful.

Flickr: It has become one of our main tool in design and prototyping phase.  Images are the best way to communicate your exact idea. We even use it to replace our discussion app, since we like to post a lot of images in the discussions. Use powerful and rich features that Flickr provides for your collaboration will give your workflow a speed and creativity boost.

Gtalk: A lot of all-in-one package provides group chat web client that allows you to host a live discussion for all your team members. You can definitely use the one comes with the package for live discussion purpose. But I strongly recommend using Gtalk as your IM tool and PartyChat (it’s a gtalk bot) for group chatting. One Gtalk feature that rules out every other IM tool is that google keeps your message history FOREVER and you can search in Gmail for whatever information in your chats. That is like a safety net. Sometimes you may have forgot to put a design decision in the wiki after the discussion and later you can’t remember the details. But as long as you can link other clue such as the when, who, topic keyword, you will easily find it in the Gmail search result. I also like to use it as part of the daily workflow. When we have big discussion about something, I will not make any note. Instead when the discussion is over, or when the other day I finally get time to update design documents, I simply load Gmail web app. Search the topic keyword and extract the useful information from message history to our wiki. Love it!

PartyChat: A Gtalk bot that provides reliable group chat feature. Gtalk web client does have the ability to host group chat, but it’s super un-reliable. People drop their connections left and right, missing message all over the place. PartyChat on the other hand never miss a single message so far for us. Also you can do group chat via PartyChat bot no matter what client of Gtalk you’re using, including any mobile client. Basically this combination allow you talk to your team mates wherever you are, getting information you need even your team mate is on vacation :p

Conclusion

That’s all, the list basically covers all the web-apps we’ve used and some are still being used. Use the list as a reference if you need to upgrade your workflow. There’s no absolute better tool or worse tool, a good tool is the one that perfectly fit the user’s hand. Think about what your team needs exactly, do your research before start to put all your stuff in, and remember, there’re plenty of free web apps out there for small teams so don’t pay unless you’re 100% sure!
