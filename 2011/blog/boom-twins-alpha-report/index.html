Boom Twins Alpha Report: What We’ve Learnt

Written on March 12, 2011 by nantas in Blog

As I wrote in the previous post, we finished our alpha test for Boom Twins last Friday. I will first post up some tips and tricks for iOS app test build online distribution. Then there’re sections listed the major feedback and what we’ve learnt from the making of the alpha version.

[heading h="2"]Distribution[/heading]

First of all, figuring out all the work flow making and distributing a game build for iOS, as well as releasing a test version online (thanks to Testflight) is a valuable experience. We started the project without any knowledge on developing app for iOS. Usually nobody will even think about figure out stuff like that when the project is far from finish. We learnt a lot from this alpha version. Although there are still a lot of issues when you start dealing with app store, at least we are half way through all the tech difficulties. Here are some very useful information that helped us during the alpha release:

How to create an IPA from TestFlight

This is actually a pretty comprehensive guide about how to build your game for online distribution. There’s only one point missing in the above guide (an important one for beginners like us): the distribution profile you used for build and archive, and then used for “Save to disk…” must contain all the device ids you want to distribute the build on. That should be common sense for most developers but easily confused for newbies.

Unable to copy dSYM file into archive

Then we ran into this error message when trying to “Build and Archive”. It’s kinda funny how I try to look for a guide in apple’s developer center first, then search for “build and archive” in google, then “Xcode build error blah blah”… Finally I throw “Unable to copy dSYM file into archive” to google and he immediately points me to the almighty stackOverflow. Problem solved. Whenever there’s an error message or exception is thrown by some library or middleware, it’s best to just google the exact word of the message.

Testing Application Name on Home Screen

This is another unknown “common knowledge” for newbie developers. iOS home screen can only display a maximum of 11 characters of an app’s name. If you are off the limit, your app name will be fucked into 2 pieces and stuffed with “…” in between. That’s why our game is called “Boom Twins” now but the title image still says “Boom Boom Twins” haha. If I would’ve known it I will not make that title image at all, or the name and title image style of the game will change completely.

Anyway we found out only after we set up all the product name properly in Unity and Xcode. If you don’t want to have a identity crisis the day before you submit your app, it’s better to test the name out way before. The link above is a very simple but practical method. Yeah I know you can just write the name on paper and count the characters but still it’s better to look and feel it on iOS home screen first IMO.

[heading h="2"]Feedback About the Game[/heading]

We invited and recruited 12 testers for Boom Twins alpha. Most of them are old colleagues who’re still working in the industry (for mainstream AAA titles), also we have some friends and families joined as well. It has been a good combination of different feedback sources that we need to take into consideration. Usually industry veterans have higher standard about the quality of the game, while normal users will provide more useful information about the accessibility to gameplay mechanics.

First I will write down each of the major problems we gathered from the feedback, followed by our opinion about the issue.

  • Control scheme (especially for single player) has got the most of  negative feedback. A lot of testers think the controls (virtual sticks and buttons) are not responsive enough. Also when it comes to active reload in the single player mode, most of the testers has trouble to get back to shooting stick after reload.

We agree with them, especially the reload button is completely off position at the moment. It is ok to let player’s right hand in charge of a couple of buttons (not recommended for action game either!), but controlling a shooting stick plus other buttons is definitely not the way to go. To solve this issue, we plan to take the idea from Infinity Field, that release your finger from the screen also counts as a user move. So we can let user touch the shooting stick to fire, and release the stick to reload.

  • For single player mode, testers are confused by the dual characters set up. Tester assumes that he need to control one of the characters but not both. One of them suggested player should be able to choose which character to play on the fly for single player mode, and let AI control the other. (take GunsBro as an example)

It’s tough to see the basic setup confuses player but we still need to deal with it. The concept of Boom Twins is actually made for multiplayer. Back then I was so fascinated by the two player coop play of Bejeweled 2 on iPad. (It was never official, just the game can be played by multiple people and really fun to be played that way.) I made a very wrong assumption that iPad game should focus on multiplayer mode because no other device or platform provides the same convenient way. Although there are titles like “Shoot shoot shot” purely about multiplayer, the market is still ruled by single player mode. iPad users usually will not bring the device around and have fun with friends, but more often just use it at home.

That means we need to reconfigure the balance between two modes and come up with a design a lot more friendly for single player. The AI controlled partner suggestion actually  is very good, we will definitely consider doing it.

  • The art style is not consistent. It’s way darker in the title screen but looks toony in the real game.

I have to admit that other than the awesome main character design, we struggled quite a lot trying to pull together zombies and overall art style. At the beginning we have the ambition to target the game market not only to core gamers, but also light users especially female players. One of the core idea about the project is to make a shooter game that can be played by couples. Because we let two players share the dual stick control, it’s much easier to let female players get into the shooter world and maybe taught the basic ideas by her friend. So at the beginning the art style is more cartoony and friendly to female users.

When we realized that the current market is not ready for a multiplayer focused game – so that not many female users will likely pick up the game at all – we start to consider the game back to the track of a hardcore shooter game. Then we want to make the game a little bit more cult like, by making the kids killing zombies in a much more gored way. Although the idea is not yet implemented (you can only sense it from the title image), we are still seemingly lost.

  • Lack of content. (more weapons, more zombie types and more Boss enemy!)

It’s called alpha but to be honest this build is only a core gameplay demo. We can for sure add up content to make it more playable, but still this requires a lot of work and we have to make sure the core gameplay is fun enough before start adding up content.  This is an issue spotted by both industry veterans and normal users. While most veterans think the lack of content is not too bad compare to the lack of emphasize on the core features, normal users want additional content more than anything else.

  • The pace is not fast enough, also picking up ammo is not necessary for a shoot-em-up.

I’m open to this opinion, but still it depends on the overall style. If it’s arcade like fast moving, tons of enemy to eliminate, then of course we can speed things up and remove ammo consumption. We are still not 100% sure about the style. As I said at the beginning there’s a lot of design made for light users. For example enemy not moving too fast, there’re not too many zombies on the same screen (good for skinning mesh performance xD), and zombie drops ammo to make sure player not standing still.

[heading h="2"]What We’ve Learnt[/heading]

First of all, the control scheme should be tested way earlier. Actually for a shooting or action game the control scheme is pretty much the core. Besides, we shouldn’t design the multiplayer first. Believe or not, most of the iPad user don’t have a friend or family to play such a shooting game with. Their girlfriend would rather watching them play Angry Birds or Cut the Rope, than play a co-op shooting game together.

Only 3 of our 12 testers have played multiplayer mode with their family/friend. Let’s face it: today it’s easy to let girls play a video game but only very few of them want to play a shooter.

Then it’s the consistency. We should stick to one style of gameplay and design features accordingly. After the fail of pitching the game as a “play with your girlfriend shooter”, guess we’d better make it arcade like, fast pace and tons of zombie and gore.

[heading h="2"]What’s Next[/heading]

Nobody think the game is totally crap. But also most of them are not impressed. That means we need to completely re-evaluate the design beats.

We will probably keep the multiplayer design but definitely re-design the single player to make the core gameplay solid enough. If we do that we will need 2 month to make another prototype and release for a test again. And if everything goes right, we still need another 3 month to polish and adding content. That’s too long and too risky.

Before we made and released the alpha build, we had decided that if we can’t finish the game in 2 month after all, we will freeze this project and move on to a much smaller scale project first. Some may disagree but I don’t think it’s a good idea to stick to something that would not impress people.

We started Boom Twins as a training project but as it went on we got higher expectation and wanted to make it to the app store. But in reality, the core mechanic is not solid and target market is evaluated totally incorrect. That’s when to call a timeout.

We really appreciate all the input from you guys, especially the ones that encourage us to finish the game. But still we’re doing this for business not for hobby. It’s also very tough for us but all the experience we got (especially remote collaboration work-flow wise) in this 7 months, as well as the alpha test feedback are most valuable for us.

We already started moving on, new project information will be posted soon.

Love you all,

 

aBit Games Team
